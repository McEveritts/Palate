# 🥩 Calvados-Basted Double Pork Chop with Caramelized Fennel 🥩

The butter foams, hissing aggressively as it hits the smoking cast-iron surface. A bouquet of bruised thyme and crushed garlic hits the hot fat, releasing a volatile, aromatic cloud that immediately commands the kitchen. Here, in the pan, a thick heritage pork chop begins its transformation from an austere, intimidating cut of meat into a caramelized, tender centerpiece, its fat rendering slowly into liquid gold. 

Pork has spent the last half-century as a victim of culinary anxiety, systematically cooked to a dry, chalky ruin in mid-century domestic kitchens. However, looking through the historical lens of the Normandy region of France, pork is treated with the same reverence as a prime dry-aged beef. The key lies in understanding the synergy between rich, marbled animal fat and the acidic, fruity bite of apples and brandy. When treated to the fierce heat of a skillet and a proper butter-basting ritual (arroser), the proteins break down gracefully rather than seizing.

This week's curation presents a thesis on regional alchemy and temperature manipulation. We are taking the accessible "other white meat" and pairing it with sides that manipulate starch and acidity. By mastering the Maillard reaction and pan-sauce emulsion in a simple skillet, we reclaim pork's rightful place on the elevated dining table, proving that true refinement is simply the application of exact technique to humble ingredients.

### 🛒 Ingredients
*   **The Protein:** 2 (1.5-inch thick) bone-in center-cut pork chops, patted dry 🥩
*   **The Seasoning:** 1 tbsp kosher salt, generously applied 🧂
*   **Aromatics:** 3 sprigs fresh thyme, lightly bruised 🌿
*   **Aromatics:** 2 cloves garlic, smashed with the skin on 🧄
*   **The Base:** 1 medium fennel bulb, cored and thinly sliced 🧅
*   **The Deglaze:** 1/3 cup Calvados (or apple brandy), at room temperature 🥃
*   **The Finish:** 3 tbsp cold unsalted butter, cubed 🧈
*   **The Finish:** 2 tbsp crème fraîche, room temperature 🥛

---

### 👨‍🍳 Culinary Execution

I. **The Maillard Sear**
Place a large cast-iron skillet over medium-high heat until it begins to lightly smoke. Add a high-smoke-point oil (like grapeseed). Place the heavily salted pork chops in the pan, pressing down firmly for maximum surface contact. Sear undisturbed for 4 minutes until a deeply mahogany crust forms. Flip the chops. **Technique Note:** The Maillard reaction requires the absence of moisture. Patting your chops aggressively dry with paper towels before hitting the pan is non-negotiable for achieving a steakhouse-level crust.

II. **The Arroser (Basting Process)**
Lower the heat to medium. Push the chops to the top of the pan. Drop in the butter, thyme, and smashed garlic at the bottom of the pan where the heat pools. As the butter melts and begins to foam, tilt the pan slightly toward you and continuously spoon the foaming butter over the meat for 2-3 minutes. **Crucial Step:** The butter must foam but not burn. If the milk solids begin turning black instead of nutty brown, pull the pan off the heat immediately to drop the temperature. Remove chops when they reach an internal temperature of 135°F, allowing carryover cooking to bring them to a perfectly juicy 140°F while resting.

III. **The Emulsified Pan Sauce**
With the chops resting on a board, drain all but 1 tablespoon of fat from the pan. Add the sliced fennel and sauté over medium heat until deeply caramelized and soft (about 5 minutes). Pull the pan completely off the flame, pour in the Calvados, and return to medium heat. Scrape the fond from the bottom of the pan as the alcohol reduces by half. Swirl in the crème fraîche and any resting juices from the pork chops until a glossy, opaque sauce forms.

---

### 💡 Chef's Additions & Troubleshooting

*   **The Flambé Risk:** When adding high-proof alcohol like Calvados to a hot pan, there is a risk of ignition. Always pull the skillet completely off the open flame before pouring to prevent a grease fire.
*   **Textural Contrast:** Garnish the finished plate with a handful of the reserved fennel fronds (the feathery green tops), lightly tossed in lemon juice and olive oil. This brings a raw, anise-forward crunch that cuts the richness of the crème fraîche.
